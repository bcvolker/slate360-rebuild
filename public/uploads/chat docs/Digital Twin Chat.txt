File Types, Uploads, and Limitations
To build a universal pipeline, your backend (the hidden CEO route we discussed) must act as a funnel that normalizes messy field data into standard formats for the AI engine.


Photos (High-Res & Drone): .jpg, .jpeg, .png, .dng (RAW is best if your backend can process it, but JPG is standard).

The Sensor Fusion Pipeline: How it all Aligns
You described walking the exterior/interior with a phone, then a 360 camera, then flying a drone. Here is exactly how the cloud aligns that chaotic mix of data:
The core technology is Structure from Motion (SfM) using software like COLMAP.
Feature Matching: The AI looks for millions of high-contrast points (a corner of a brick, a scratch on the floor). It matches that exact scratch in a drone photo, a phone photo, and a 360-video frame.
The "Bridge": To connect the inside of the building to the outside, you must walk through open doors or windows with your camera. The AI uses those transition frames to physically lock the exterior drone model to the interior 360 model.
The Scale Anchor: As mentioned in previous chats, photogrammetry is "scale-less." By throwing your iPhone LiDAR (.ply) scan into the mix, the engine uses it as a "Depth Prior." It forces the massive drone/360 model to snap to the exact metric dimensions (inches/centimeters) of your phone's LiDAR scan.
Quality, Distortions, and Artifact Removal


360 Video Distortion: 360 cameras use an "equirectangular projection" (it looks like a flattened, warped world map). Will this ruin measurements? No. Modern engines (like Nerfstudio) have specific mathematical camera models that perfectly "un-warp" equirectangular images during training.
Removing Yourself: If you walk with a camera on a stick, you will be in the model. As discussed previously, you write a Python script in your cloud worker that automatically places a black circular "mask" over the bottom 15% (the Nadir) of every 360 frame. The AI ignores the black mask and perfectly reconstructs the floor beneath you using other camera angles.


CAD & As-Built Integration
You do not feed CAD files into the photogrammetry engine to improve the model. The engine's job is to capture reality as it exists right now. CAD is design intent.
However, you do bring them together in your Slate360 WebGL Viewer.


Design vs. Reality: You load the generated Gaussian Splat, and then you load the architect's .ifc or .glb CAD file into the same 3D viewer.
The Value: Users can manually align them to perform "Clash Detection." They can see that the concrete contractor poured a column 4 inches to the left of where the CAD file said it should be. This is a highly monetizable feature.
Exporting for Architects: To get your twin into an architect's software (Revit, Navisworks), your cloud worker must export the final model as an .e57, .las (Point Cloud), or an .obj/.fbx (Mesh).


The Truth About "Centimeter Accuracy" & Certification
This is the most dangerous trap for reality-capture startups.


Relative vs. Absolute Accuracy: Your model might have incredible relative accuracy (if I measure a window in the viewer, it is exactly 36 inches wide). But its absolute/global accuracy (where that window sits on the planet Earth) might be off by 5 feet if you only used standard drone GPS.
Who Certifies This? In the US, the ASPRS (American Society for Photogrammetry and Remote Sensing) sets the standards. However, legally, only a Licensed Professional Land Surveyor (PLS) can legally certify that a map or model is geographically accurate.
How do they sample it? They do not check the whole model. They place 10 physical targets (Ground Control Points - GCPs) on the site. The surveyor measures them with a $30,000 Total Station. You run your drone model. You compare your model's coordinates for those 10 targets against the surveyor's coordinates. The software spits out an RMSE (Root Mean Square Error) report.
Is it worth it? For you, right now? Absolutely not. Hiring a surveyor to stamp your models costs thousands of dollars per site and drastically slows down your workflow.


Marketing Strategy: Workflow vs. Survey-Grade
DO NOT market your platform as "Survey-Grade" or legally accurate. If an architect uses your model to pour a foundation and it is off by 2 inches, you will be sued.
Market it as "High-Fidelity Visual Context and Workflow Acceleration." * Your competitive advantage is not that you replace a surveyor. Your advantage is that you are replacing Matterport (which is stuck indoors) and DroneDeploy (which struggles indoors) with a Unified, Seamless Indoor-Outdoor Digital Twin.


The Pitch: "Slate360 Twins provide unparalleled visual context, allowing stakeholders to virtually walk the site, take rapid relative measurements, verify design-intent against CAD, and communicate through interactive pins—saving your team hours of driving to the site every week."
Include a standard legal disclaimer in your viewer: "Measurements are approximate and for visual reference/coordination only. Not to be used for structural engineering or legal property boundaries."

The Magic of "Clipping Planes" (How Slicing Works)
When you want to cut a building in half to see the interior layout, we do not permanently delete the data. Instead, we use a WebGL feature called Clipping Planes (or a Section Box).


The Tech: In your React Three Fiber (R3F) viewer, we generate a mathematical, invisible plane. We instruct the graphics card: "If any part of the Gaussian Splat or the CAD model falls on the left side of this plane, make it 100% transparent."
The UI: On your desktop editor, the user clicks a "Section View" button. A translucent box with arrows appears around the model. As the user drags the top arrow down, the roof of the digital twin smoothly disappears, revealing the floor plan inside.


Aligning Slices with CAD Files (Clash Detection)
This is where you can charge clients serious money. You want to slice the building in half and see if the real-world plumbing matches the architect's CAD file.


The Workflow: The alignment happens before the slice. First, the user loads the drone/LiDAR twin. Next, they import the .glb CAD file. They use an XYZ slider (or a 3-point click system) to perfectly align the CAD design over the real-world concrete.
The Slice: Because both models exist in the exact same 3D scene, when you activate the Clipping Plane, it slices through both models simultaneously. You will be looking at a perfect cross-section where the digital CAD pipe is visibly sitting 2 inches to the right of the real-world installed pipe.


"Opening Like a Book" (Split Transforms)
Opening a building like a dollhouse or a book is a spectacular visual feature, especially for client presentations.


How it works: In the code, we actually load the model twice (Model A and Model B) exactly on top of each other. We use a Clipping Plane to hide the left half of Model A, and a reversed Clipping Plane to hide the right half of Model B.
The Hinge: We then assign a "pivot point" (a hinge) to the center edge of Model A. When the user drags a slider, Model A rotates open on that hinge, revealing the cross-section of the entire interior, perfectly aligned with the CAD overlay inside.

Field Capture Masterclass: Phoenix Sun & Stadium Rafters
The Phoenix Sun & ND Filters You mentioned that drones and 360 cameras don't have ND (Neutral Density) filters. Good news: They actually do! You can buy ND filter snap-on kits specifically made for the DJI Mavic 3 Enterprise, DJI Avata, and Insta360 X3/X4/X5.


Why you need them: The harsh Phoenix sun creates "blown-out" white areas on concrete or roofs. Blown-out pixels have zero data (pure white), meaning the AI engine (COLMAP) cannot find features to track, resulting in holes in your 3D model.
If you don't have ND filters: Yes, underexposing by -1.0 or -1.5 EV is the correct move. The AI can extract shadows, but it cannot invent data from pure white glare. Crucial: Lock your white balance and exposure manually. If the camera auto-adjusts from light to dark as you walk, the AI will create floating, foggy artifacts.

The Solution: You must fly the drone around, under, and above the rafters. The 600mm ground photos can be thrown into the same dataset—the AI will use them to apply hyper-realistic textures to the underside—but the drone is required to build the actual 3D geometry of the roof.
